Water Related Careers

Water is a precious resource in South Africa, and thousands of people work in the industry to manage it. With many years of experience and expertise in this field, South Africa has become a leader in water technology and services. Water-based careers are a great choice for those who love being around water.

Agricultural Engineer

Agricultural engineers are the individuals that deal with problems that concern the agricultural sector such as the power supply, machine efficiency, pollution, environmental issues, and the storage and processing of agricultural products.

Career details:

They focus on the advancement of the agricultural industry. Agricultural Engineers usually work with farmers and companies that are involved in the agriculture sector. These professionals work to solve the current problems that the agricultural industry is facing for example by developing equipment that could increase production.

As an agricultural engineer, you can specialize in different areas, water supply, and irrigation, soil conservation, agricultural mechanization, food and fiber processing, and agricultural mechanization.

Skills required for this career:

  • Complex problem-solving skills
  • Design skills and knowledge
  • The ability to work well with others
  • Analytical thinking skills

Study options:

  • BSc Engineering: Agricultural
  • BSc (Agriculture) majoring in Agrometeorology with Agricultural Engineering
  • BSc (Agriculture) majoring in Soil Science with Agricultural Engineering

Possible employers:

  • Department of Agriculture
  • Manufactures of agricultural remedies and food companies
  • Manufactures of farm equipment and supplies
  • Consulting firms
  • Agricultural control boards and unions

Aquatic Biologist

Employees in this career measure the effects of water pollution on aquatic life. Aquatic biologists study the ecology of the freshwater and marine ecosystem. Their day-to-day duties include conducting field research, collecting samples, and making observations of plants and animals.

Career details

Aquatic biologists may also be referred to as fisheries biologists, ichthyologists, or marine biologists. People in this profession love working outdoors. And they specialize according to their interests, for example, some aquatic biologists study oceanic systems while others prefer to study systems such as rivers, lakes, or streams.

Aquatic biologists develop and implement systems that help protect aquatic systems. These individuals can also help influence policies and laws that benefit important aquatic systems.

Skills required for this career:

  • Strong team skills
  • Analytical and problem-solving skills
  • Good oral and written communication skills
  • Critical thinking skills

Study options:

  • B.Sc. Chemistry
  • B.Sc. Biological Sciences
  • B.Sc. Marine Biology

Possible employers:

  • Consulting firms
  • Universities
  • Municipalities
  • Museums
  • Various water boards
  • Departments of Environmental Affairs and Tourism

Aquatic Chemist

Aquatic chemists ensure that the water we drink, and use is at a safe standard. Water chemists are scientists who not only study potential pollutants in water but also analyze to see how environmental changes impact the chemical makeup of a water source.

Career details:

These professionals work in different types of locations including labs where they can perform tests on the water and in the field. Aquatic chemists spend their time collecting samples, evaluating ecosystems, and monitoring the conditions of the bodies of water in a particular area, including lakes and streams, and they also assess water that has been contaminated. Water chemistry is an interdisciplinary field, so you might find that these professionals take on responsibilities assigned to hydrologists, hydrobiogeochemists, or hydrogeologists.

Aquatic chemists usually start work in the field and as they grow in their careers, they take on higher profiles and more complex projects. Students who choose this career path should take as many courses in environmental policy as possible to help them understand how the data they collect affect public policy. It’s also important that you work on your physical stamina in this career because you might find yourself in situations where you need to walk or stand for long periods.

Skills required for this career:

  • Analytical skills
  • Critical thinking skills
  • Excellent oral and written communication
  • Complex problem-solving skills

Study options:

  • Bachelor Electrical Engineering/li>
  • Bachelor Electronic Engineering
  • B.Sc. Information Engineering
  • B.Sc. Electrical Engineering

    Possible employers:

  • Manufacturing companies
  • Telecommunications companies
  • Research and development companies

Aquatic scientist

Aquatic science is the study of the planet’s oceanic and freshwater environments.

Aquatic scientists use comparative studies, long-term data, models, and theory to address a multitude of questions concerning water

Career details:

In this career, you study marine wildlife, aquatic ecosystems, water chemistry, and more. This kind of work is best suited for those individuals who love the natural environment. Aquatic scientists often work with interdisciplinary groups. For example, Aquatic scientists would collaborate with chemists to have a better understanding of how chemicals would affect sea life.

Skills required for this career:

  • Excellent analytical skills
  • Attention to detail
  • Great communication skills
  • Critical thinking skills

Study options:

  • B.Sc. Marine Biology
  • B. Eng. Mechatronic Engineering
  • Diploma Marine Science
  • M.Sc. Aquatic Health
  • B.Sc. Zoology
  • B.Sc. Ecology

Possible employers:

  • Colleges and universities
  • Government agencies
  • Museums
  • Private companies that are involved in the ocean
  • Research institutions

Diver

This is an exciting career path to go into because these individuals play a vital role in most organizations. Divers work in a variety of industries, for example, they would get hired by large diving contracting companies to do construction and maintenance work on offshore oil rigs. Some work for law enforcement or insurance investigating firms to look for accident victims, wreckage, or lost valuables.

Career details:

Their responsibilities include performing duties underwater such as inspecting, repairing, removing, or installing equipment and structures. Diver professionals also use power and hand tools, for example, drills, sledgehammers, or welding equipment to help them perform their duties.

Other divers work with scientific research teams to photograph fish, vegetation, or minerals.

But life as a diver doesn’t always have to be serious you can always work at resorts as a recreational driver, acting as an undersea tour guide for people on holiday.

Skills required for this career:

  • CPR skills
  • Hand tools
  • Job safety analysis
  • Water construction skills

Study options:

  • B.S. Divers
  • Air/Mixed Gas Commercial Diver Program
  • Class VI, V, IV, And III Commercial Dive Training
  • 50-meter Commercial Air diver

Possible employers:

  • Museums
  • Water transportation companies
  • Law Departments
  • Insurance companies

Geographic Information System Specialist

A GIS specialist builds and maintains GIS databases. A GIS system is a computer-based method for analyzing and displaying geographically referenced information. These professionals may also be called, GIS analysts, GIS technicians, and cartographers.

Career details:

GIS specialists are highly trained individuals that spend their time creating maps that show political borders, rivers, and environmental resources.

Their daily duties also include entering data such as texts or spreadsheet files of latitude and longitude coordinates, tabular data, satellite imagery, and manual digitization of paper maps or map images.

Skills required for this career:

  • Excellent problem-solving skills
  • Good interpersonal skills
  • Project management skills
  • Leadership skills

Study options:

  • Bachelor in Cartographer
  • Bachelor in Geography and Environmental Management
  • B.Sc. Applied Geology
  • B.Sc. Engineering and Environmental Geology
  • Bachelor in Nautical Science
  • B.Sc. Geological science
  • B.Sc. Geomatics

Possible employers:

  • Government departments
  • Oil and gas industries
  • Private industries
  • Environment non-profits

Hydrogeology

A hydrogeologist is a person who studies the location, movement, and quality of water formations that are below the surface of the earth. All the research and findings that a hydrogeologist is involved in, get used by mining, construction, and manufacturing companies.

Career details

Daily hydrogeologists are involved in several duties such as investigating ways to test the water, checking the licenses of people who construct wells, and supplying water to others. Inspect the design and function of a well, and hydrogeologists study the groundwater, and how it moves through the soil.

At times hydrogeologists can also be involved in the clean-up of spills and contamination in the water.

Skills required for this career:

  • An excellent level of numeracy
  • Project management skills
  • Excellent teamwork abilities
  • Good verbal and written communication skills

Study options:

  • B.Sc. Hydrology
  • B.Sc. Environmental and Water Science
  • B.Sc. Geology
  • B.Sc. Earth Sciences

Possible employers:

  • Environmental and engineering consultancies
  • Research organizations
  • Water supply companies
  • Mining organizations
  • Waste disposal companies
  • Renewable energy companies

Hydrographic Surveyor

A hydrographic surveyor is a professional who measures and maps the world’s underwater surfaces and studies the construction of the seabed. They specialize in precise positioning, data acquisition, and processing in onshore or offshore marine environments.

Career details

As a hydrographic surveyor, you will be working with planners or civil engineers to monitor and protect the environment during construction projects. Studying inland waters, rivers, ports, and oceans. And collecting information about the type of seabed along with the movement of water and waves.

The role of a hydrographic surveyor is to use specialized equipment and techniques to make charts and maps to describe how the sea floor, riverbeds, and shores physically look like.

When you go into this career path depending on the organization you want to work for, you can become a hydrographic survey with just your matric, but for you to grow in this role, you must study a four-year degree, and there are several degrees you can get, that will help you in your role as a hydrographic surveyor.

Skills required for this career:

  • Practical problem-solving skills
  • To be a logical thinker
  • Aware of safety issues
  • Ability to make decisions

Study options:

  • M.Sc. Computer Science
  • M.Sc. Geography
  • B.Sc. Hydrology
  • Bachelor Oceanography
  • Bachelor Mine Surveying

Possible employers:

  • Port and harbor authorities
  • Defense navies
  • Equipment and software companies
  • Freelance surveyors consultancies
  • Organizations involved in land reclamation
  • Coastal protection agencies

Hydrologist

Hydrologists undertake both office and fieldwork. They are responsible for studying water properties on the earth’s surface and underground and they resolve water-related issues.

Career details

Hydrologists improve the quality and quantity of water in given areas, and they forecast floods, snowstorms, and droughts. They also work on finding new water sources. People in this career produce water and drought management plans. They record and analyze water resource system data and search for underground water.

Hydrologists must have strong teamwork abilities because they often have to work with other scientists to help preserve and clean up the environment.

Skills required for this career:

  • Project management skills
  • Excellent organizational skills
  • Ability to work in a team
  • Good problem-solving skills

Study options:

  • B.Sc. Hydrology
  • B.Sc. Environmental Sciences and Water Sciences
  • Bachelor in engineering – Civil (Water Engineering)

Possible employers:

  • Environmental agencies
  • Water or sewage companies
  • Research and development organization
  • Government departments

Irrigation Specialist

Irrigation specialists are employees that are not only responsible for designing and installing irrigation systems, but they ensure that the irrigation system works efficiently and conserves water by inspecting, auditing, adjusting, and repairing it.

Career details:

Irrigation specialists work with a variety of different equipment, at times you may find that they install water timers with heavy machinery. And because you work most of the time outdoors, you might be exposed to extreme weather temperatures, dirt, dust, fumes, or even unpleasant odors.

Irrigation specialists perform many duties, they monitor water levels in wells and reservoirs to ensure enough water is available for irrigation purposes. They install or repair drainage systems to prevent flooding. They conduct research and find additional sources of water for irrigation purposes.

Skills required for this career:

  • Good verbal and written communication skills
  • Ability to read blueprints
  • Leadership skills
  • Excellent problem-solving skills

Study options:

  • Bachelor in Agricultural Science
  • B.Sc. Soil Science
  • B.Sc. Botany
  • National Certificate: Landscape Irrigation

Possible employers:

  • Government departments
  • Nonprofit organizations
  • Farms
  • Research institutions
  • Private companies
  • Landscaping companies
  • Golf courses

Waste Management Researcher

As a waste manager researcher, you are responsible for developing waste and pollution reduction solutions. You must make sure organizations and households get proper disposal services on specific days as planned.

Career details:

Other duties include analyzing statistics concerning local waste levels, recycling, and making appropriate recommendations. Waste manager researchers develop and update policy procedures on waste management. They also make sure that the departments meet landfill diversion, waste reduction, and recycling goals.

Once students have obtained their studies in this broad field, they can choose to specialize in fields they are more interested in, such as landfill design and management, management of industrial and organic waste, or design of water/wastewater treatment plants.

Skills required for this career:

  • Good analytical skills
  • Strong organizational skills

Study options:

  • Solid Waste Management
  • B.Sc. Environmental Studies

Possible employers:

  • Municipalities
  • Disposal companies
  • Large industrial firms
  • Consulting companies
  • Emergency response companies
  • Government departments

Water Allocation Officer

Water allocation officers evaluate the present and future water demands and ensure that enough water is accessible for commercial and domestic use. People in this career monitor the allocation and use of water from dams, rivers, and reservoirs.

Career details:

Water allocation officers oversee the urban and rural water systems, and they use their knowledge of water guidelines, laws, policies, and regulations to advise on the distribution and allocation of water to communities. Employees in this profession are also responsible for assessing water use license applications and permits.

Skills required for this career:

  • Strong problem-solving ability
  • Analytical skills with excellent critical thinking capacity
  • Good communication and negotiation skills
  • Good interpersonal skills

Study options:

  • B.Sc. Hydrology
  • B.Sc. Environmental Science (specializing in water science)
  • B.Sc. Environmental and Water Science
  • B.Sc. Hydrology and Water Resource Management

Possible employers:

  • Government departments
  • Water Utility companies
  • Environmental agencies
  • Research and development organizations

Water Control Officer

Water control officers are responsible for managing water distribution to water users. They perform minor maintenance on structures, dams, fences, and sluices. They evaluate the distribution of water supply to water users, supervise and audit water use dates, and register all water users.

Career details:

Water control officers are also involved in training in water measurement and distribution. People in this career must also have basic knowledge of civil, mechanical, and electrical maintenance.

Skills required for this career:

  • Good verbal and written communication skills
  • Good problem-solving skills
  • Effective team working skills
  • Good Interpersonal skills

Study options:

  • National Diploma Water Quality Management Production
  • Bachelor’s Water Quality Management Production
  • M.Sc. Engineering (specializing in water quality engineering)

Possible employers:

  • Government departments
  • Water utility companies

Water Inspector

Water inspectors are employees that ensure water is safe for consumption and public use, by making sure that water contaminants are kept within safe limits.

Career details:

Water inspectors are involved in the development of water allocation plans to protect the ecosystems that depend on water resources such as wetlands and rivers. Water inspectors, do evaluations, inspections, and sampling activities for water treatment facilities, dams, reservoirs, and refreshment bottling companies.

Water inspectors may also be involved in the investigation of water contamination issues should they take.

Skills required for this career:

  • Pay attention to the detail
  • Critical thinking skills
  • System analysis skills
  • Good verbal and written communication

Study options:

  • Diploma in Built Environment in Construction Studies
  • Bachelors Civil Engineering

Possible employers:

  • Government departments
  • Water and waste services
  • Construction companies

Water Liaison Practitioner

Water liaison practitioners promote sustainable water use and they implement and develop communication strategies by providing clear and concise water-related information.

Career details:

Water liaison practitioners analyze legislation and policies related to water. And they organize water-related events to establish, maintain and promote relationships and partnerships among water users.

Water liaison practitioners must have strong networking and communications skills because they often consult with public and private stakeholders, scientists, and businesses to promote sustainable water use.

Skills required for this career:

  • Excellent networking skills
  • Good communication and presentation skills
  • Good project management skills
  • Strong problem-solving skills

Study options:

  • B.Sc. Environmental Science (Specializing in water science)
  • B.Sc. Environmental and Water Science
  • Diploma in Public Relations and Communication
  • Diploma in Water Science and Technology

Possible employers:

  • Government departments
  • Catchment management agencies
  • Water Utility Companies

Water Resource Manager

Water resource managers’ main role is to make sure that the environmental needs and the economic needs are in balance.

Career details:

They understand both the needs of the economy and the needs of the environment which make them the perfect people to keep the balance. Water resource managers oversee duties such as assessing environmental and economic impacts. These individuals have substantial knowledge of policy and planning law. People in this career oversee the conservation, study, and treatment of local water sources. Water resource managers make sure that the water is safe for humans and animals to use and that businesses are using water sustainably.

People in this career furthermore create conservation plans and strategies for specific water sources. And besides approving water usage for companies and individuals to ensure there is a balance kept between the environment and our human needs, they also educate organizations and people on water sustainability.

Skills required for this career:

  • Good verbal and written communication skills
  • GIS skills
  • Complex problem-solving skills
  • Leadership skills

Study options:

  • B.Sc. Biological Science
  • B.Sc. Chemical Engineering
  • B.Sc. Mechanical Engineering
  • B.Sc. Chemistry

Possible employers:

  • Engineering firms
  • Government departments
  • Environmental agencies

Water Treatment Plant Operator

Individuals in this position are responsible for the day-to-day operations of the plant. They ensure that the water is treated properly before it gets sent out to customers by monitoring and controlling the equipment.

Career details:

Water treatment plant operators learn specific skills and knowledge they need for their role on the job. Their duties include operating and maintaining water treatment plant equipment, keeping an accurate record of plant operations, adhere to all safety protocols and procedures when working with chemicals. They have very strong customer service skills which enable them to communicate effectively with customers about using sustainable products.

The water treatment plants are usually located in rural areas near the water source. And operators may be exposed to hazardous chemicals and may work in confined spaces.

To be successful in this career learners must have a strong foundation in science, maths, and engineering. And you must be comfortable working with complex systems.

Skills required for this career:

  • Excellent communication skills
  • Good Problem-solving skills
  • Attention to detail
  • Strong customer service skills

Study options:

  • National Diploma Water Care
  • Higher Certificate Resource and Waste Management
  • Advanced Certificate Water Treatment
  • National Certificate in Water Care

Possible employers:

  • Water purification plants
  • Government departments
  • Municipalities
  • Water utility companies

Water Use Specialist

Water use specialists are people who evaluate and monitor the use of water.

Career details:

To make sure that they monitor the use of water properly they conduct surveys and collect and analyze water samples to regulate changes in water supply and quality. The main task of a water use specialist is to ensure communities receive enough water and that the water supply is safe for use. These individuals also create proposals to increase water availability in areas that are lacking.

Water use specialists can also get involved in the investigation of water problems in given areas. They investigate issues such as pollution or other issues that could affect the health of the water. These specialists work in a variety of settings like the laboratory, office, or field. They sometimes spend a lot of time outdoors in all types of weather conditions.

Skills required for this career:

  • Complex problem-solving skills
  • Critical thinking skills
  • Attention to detail
  • Good communication skills

Study options:

  • Bachelor in Chemistry
  • B.Sc. Ecology
  • B.Sc. Geography
  • B.Sc. Hydrology

Possible employers:

  • Engineering firms
  • Environmental consulting firms
  • Government departments
  • Water Utility companies

Water and Wastewater Engineers

Water and wastewater engineers are involved in building, repairing, and maintaining structures that control water resources, such as sea defense walls, pumping stations, and reservoirs.

Career details:

TThey also monitor flood levels and maintain awareness of environmental issues. Wastewater Engineers must know about public safety and health laws to ensure that wastewater and hazardous chemicals are handled and disposed of properly. Their typical duties include producing designs for sewage, water treatment, and flood defense structures.

  • Excellent problem-solving skills
  • Excellent verbal and written communication
  • Ability to work in a team

  • B.Sc. Civil Engineering

Water Quality Analyst

Water is a very important part of our well-being and the quality of water we use must be of a healthy standard. Water quality analysts develop purification and treatment procedures to ensure the quality and safety standards of water being used for drinking or agricultural use are met.

Career details:

Individuals in this career clean wastewater and they do this by manipulating the pumps, valves, and other equipment to remove impurities from the water.

Water quality analysts perform several duties such as collecting and analyzing water samples, and they develop and coordinate the implementation of environmental management systems. They also conduct audits to evaluate the environmental impacts of existing activities.

Skills required for this career:

  • Good problem-solving skills
  • Good attention to detail
  • Organizational skills
  • Critical thinking skills

Study options:

  • National Diploma in Water Care
  • B.Sc. Biology
  • B.Sc. Chemistry
  • B.Sc. Environmental Science

Possible employers:

  • Mining companies
  • Water boards
  • Department of water affairs
  • Chemical manufacturing industries
  • Eskom
  • Sasol
  • Municipalities
